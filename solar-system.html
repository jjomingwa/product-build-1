Interactive Solar System Gravity Simulator

This interactive tool allows students and enthusiasts to explore the gravitational dynamics of a planetary system. Unlike static textbook diagrams, this N-body simulation calculates the forces between celestial bodies in real-time, providing a visceral understanding of orbital mechanics.

1. The Law of Universal Gravitation

At the heart of this simulation is Isaac Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation. Every point mass attracts every other point mass by a force acting along the line intersecting the two points. The force is proportional to the product of the two masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them:

F = G * (m1 * m2) / r^2

In our simulation, the central "Sun" (yellow) is a massive object that dominates the gravitational field. The "Planets" (orange and blue) are initialized with specific velocities that allow them to fall around the sun in stable orbits, balancing their forward inertia against the inward pull of gravity.

2. Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion

By observing the simulation, you can verify Kepler's three laws:

  • First Law (The Law of Ellipses): The path of the planets about the sun is elliptical in shape, with the center of the sun being located at one focus. You can see that the orbits are not perfect circles.
  • Second Law (The Law of Equal Areas): An imaginary line drawn from the center of the sun to the center of the planet will sweep out equal areas in equal intervals of time. Notice how the planets speed up when they are closer to the sun (perihelion) and slow down when they are further away (aphelion).
  • Third Law (The Law of Harmonies): The ratio of the squares of the periods of any two planets is equal to the ratio of the cubes of their average distances from the sun. The inner planet orbits much faster than the outer one.

3. Gravitational Potential Fields

The background particles in this simulation visualize the Gravitational Field. The field intensity is stronger closer to the massive bodies. The vectors (lines) show the direction a small test mass would accelerate if placed at that point. You can see how the field lines "bend" towards the massive objects, illustrating the concept that gravity is a curvature of space-time, as proposed by Einstein in his General Theory of Relativity.

4. The N-Body Problem

This simulation is a practical example of the "N-Body Problem". While predicting the motion of two bodies is solved analytically (Kepler/Newton), adding a third body makes the system chaotic. We use numerical integration (specifically a variation of the Velocity Verlet algorithm) to calculate the positions step-by-step. This method is crucial for maintaining energy stability over long simulation times.

5. How to Use This Tool for Learning

Teachers can use this tool to demonstrate:

  • Why planets don't fly off into space or crash into the sun.
  • The concept of "escape velocity" (try using the Right Click to repel particles!).
  • The perturbation of orbits caused by other planets (observe the slight wobbles).
